Climber

How the Send Score works

One number for how you're climbing — a rating, like a chess Elo. It rises when you send above your level, holds steady at the grades you cruise, and settles at your current climbing level.

First, the words climbers use

SendYou climbed it to the top clean — no falls, no resting on the rope. It still counts no matter how many tries it took to get there.
FlashA send on your very first try — maybe after watching a friend do it or getting tips on the moves.
ProjectA climb you tried but haven't finished yet. Logging projects is a good thing — working something too hard for you is how you get better.
GradesHow hard a climb is. Boulders use V0–V17; roped routes use 5.5–5.15. Bigger number = harder. Your rating sits on the same scale — a beginner on easy grades starts near 1000, and every grade is worth about 100 points.

How the number moves

Send above your level and it climbsEach climb is scored against your rating, like a chess match against the route. Send something hard for you and the number jumps; cruise things you're expected to send and it barely moves — so you can't inflate it with easy laps.
It settles at your levelKeep climbing the same grades and your rating levels off right around them — it reflects how you climb now, not how much you climb. Break into a harder grade and it rises to a new plateau. Volume alone never carries you up.
Trying hard is nearly freeFalling on a project above your level barely dents the rating — you weren't expected to send it. Only backing off climbs below your usual level costs much. Flashing something earns a touch extra, so projecting and flashing both pay off.
Finding your levelYour first few sessions are marked "provisional" and move faster while the rating homes in on where you belong. The ▲/▼ beside a score is how many points that climber's latest session moved it.
